Overview

We implemented the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs. During simulation, the system accumulates external and spring forces, integrates the point masses forward with Verlet integration, enforces Provot's 10% spring stretch constraint, handles collisions with spheres and planes, and resolves self-collisions with a spatial hash to keep the simulation interactive.

The most interesting takeaway was how much qualitative behavior comes from only a few parameters. Even with a simple mass-spring model, varying stiffness, density, damping, and collision handling produces very different folds, draping, and settling behavior.

Part 1: Masses and springs

In Cloth::buildGrid, we first generated a row-major grid of point masses over the cloth domain. For horizontal cloth, the masses lie on the xz-plane with constant y = 1. For vertical cloth, the masses lie on the xy-plane and each point mass receives a small random z offset in the range [-0.001, 0.001] to break symmetry. Pinned masses are marked by checking whether their grid coordinate appears in the input pinned list.

After all point masses were created, we added springs for the three required constraint types. Structural springs connect each point mass to the mass above and to the left. Shearing springs connect to the upper-left and upper-right diagonals. Bending springs connect to the mass two positions above and two positions to the left. Adding springs only after the full grid exists keeps the point-mass pointers stable and makes the row-major indexing straightforward.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In Cloth::simulate, we begin each step by resetting every point mass's accumulated force and adding the total external force derived from all accelerations. We then iterate over the springs and apply Hooke's law based on the spring extension \( \|p_a - p_b\| - l \). Structural and shearing springs use the scene stiffness \(k_s\), while bending springs use a weaker scaled stiffness to keep them less restrictive.

For integration, we used damped Verlet integration: \( x_{t+\Delta t} = x_t + (1-d)(x_t - x_{t-\Delta t}) + a_t \Delta t^2 \). Pinned masses are skipped. After integration, we enforce Provot's deformation constraint so that no spring extends beyond 110% of its rest length. The correction is split evenly between both endpoints, unless one endpoint is pinned, in which case the full correction is applied to the unpinned mass.

Increasing ks makes the cloth stiffer, so it resists stretching and settles into sharper suspended folds. Lower ks makes it sag much more and behave like a softer fabric. Increasing density increases the total mass, making gravity dominate more strongly and producing deeper, heavier drape. Increasing damping removes energy faster, so the cloth settles more quickly with less oscillation; low damping allows larger swings and longer-lived motion.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

For sphere collisions, we test whether a point mass lies inside the sphere by checking its distance from the sphere center. If it does, we project the point mass back to the sphere surface along the radial direction to find the tangent point. We then compute the correction from the previous position to that tangent point and apply only \(1 - f\) of it so friction reduces the displacement.

For plane collisions, we determine whether the point mass crossed the plane during the last step by comparing the signs of the signed distances from its current and previous positions to the plane. If the signs differ, we project the previous position onto the plane, offset that point slightly along the plane normal using SURFACE_OFFSET, and then apply the friction-scaled correction to the point mass.

With ks = 500, the cloth drapes more loosely around the sphere and conforms closely to the surface. With the default ks = 5000, it holds broader folds while still wrapping naturally. With ks = 50000, the cloth is much stiffer and bridges more strongly over the sphere, producing fewer deep wrinkles.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

To avoid the \(O(n^2)\) cost of testing every pair of point masses, we implemented spatial hashing. The method hash_position partitions space into boxes of size \( w = 3 \cdot width / num\_width\_points \), \( h = 3 \cdot height / num\_height\_points \), and \( t = \max(w, h) \). Each point mass is assigned to one box, and the integer box coordinates are combined into a hash key used in the map.

In build_spatial_map, we rebuild the hash table every time step by inserting each point mass into the vector associated with its box. Then in self_collide, each point mass only compares itself against masses in the same box. If another point mass is closer than 2 * thickness, we accumulate a correction that pushes the current point mass away just enough to restore the minimum separation. The final correction is the average over all candidates, divided by simulation_steps to keep the update stable.

Higher density causes the cloth to collapse faster and compress into heavier folds before the self-collision response pushes layers apart. Higher ks keeps the cloth more rigid, so it forms broader arches and less crumpled folds. Lower ks allows thinner, tighter wrinkles and more compact folding.

Part 5: Shaders

We implemented the core shading pipeline in GLSL. The Phong shader uses ambient, diffuse, and Blinn-Phong specular lighting based on the world space light direction, camera direction, and interpolated surface normal. The texture shader replaces the constant albedo with a sampled color texture while keeping the same lighting model. The mirror shader computes the reflected viewing direction and samples the environment cubemap.

For bump mapping, we sample the height texture in tangent space, estimate finite differences in the texture's u and v directions, and construct a perturbed local normal. We then transform that normal into world space using the TBN frame built from the tangent, bitangent, and geometric normal. Displacement mapping extends that same height field into the vertex shader by offsetting each vertex along its normal before rasterization, while the fragment shader still computes a perturbed normal for lighting.

The custom shader mixes two fabric textures with a reflective cubemap term and a Fresnel-style blend to create a stylized glossy cloth appearance that still reads as woven material under motion.

(Optional) Part 6: Extra Credit - Additional cloth simulation features!

Wind Force

We added an optional wind model that can be enabled from the scene JSON through a wind object on the cloth. The parameters control whether wind is enabled, its direction, its strength, and a frequency term. During each simulation step, every point mass receives an additional wind force in the configured direction. The force is modulated by simulation time and point-mass position, which creates moving gusts instead of a uniform constant push. A small lateral component is mixed in to create visible flutter.

Axis-Aligned Box Collision

We also added a new axis-aligned box primitive. The scene parser accepts a box object with min, max, and friction fields, and the simulator instantiates a Box collision object. For collision response, if a point mass is inside the box, we project it to the nearest box face plus a small surface offset, then apply the correction from the point mass's previous position scaled by 1 - friction. The box is also rendered as six triangle faces so it is visible in the viewer.
