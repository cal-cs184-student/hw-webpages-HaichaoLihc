Overview

In this homework, I built the core pieces of a Monte Carlo path tracer. Starting from camera ray generation and primitive intersection, I added BVH acceleration, direct lighting with both hemisphere and importance sampling, recursive global illumination with Russian Roulette, and adaptive sampling. For extra credit, I also replaced the original pure random pixel sampler with a jittered stratified sampler and compared the two sampling strategies at the same spp.

The most interesting part of the assignment was seeing how each stage changes the character of the image. Direct illumination produces a recognizable scene, but the image still feels flat. Once indirect transport is added, corners brighten, shadowed regions pick up bounced light, and the whole render feels more physically plausible. Adaptive sampling then makes the computation itself more interesting, since the renderer naturally spends more work on edges, shadow boundaries, and difficult indirect-lighting regions.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

In Part 1, I implemented the basic rendering pipeline from camera ray generation through primitive intersection. In raytrace_pixel, I sample random subpixel positions, normalize them into sensor coordinates, and pass them to Camera::generate_ray. That function maps the sample to the virtual sensor plane at z = -1, builds the camera-space ray direction, transforms it into world space with the camera-to-world matrix, and initializes the ray's clip range using min_t and max_t.

I then added ray-triangle and ray-sphere intersection. For triangles, I used the Moller-Trumbore test and interpolated the shading normal with barycentric coordinates. For spheres, I solved the quadratic equation and chose the closest valid root. In both cases, a hit updates ray.max_t, so later tests can ignore farther intersections and keep only the nearest visible surface.

The triangle routine checks whether the barycentric coordinates fall inside the triangle and whether the hit lies in the ray's valid t interval. If so, it stores the hit distance, interpolated normal, primitive pointer, and BSDF. This gives correct normal shading on both simple meshes and spheres.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

I constructed the BVH recursively in BVHAccel::construct_bvh. For each node, I compute the primitive bounding box and centroid bounding box. If the primitive count is small enough, the node becomes a leaf. Otherwise, I split the range into two subsets and recurse on both children.

My split heuristic uses the axis with the largest centroid extent and partitions at the midpoint along that axis. If the midpoint split degenerates and places all primitives on one side, I fall back to a median split with nth_element. This guarantees progress and avoids infinite recursion.

I used the slab test for ray-box intersection and then updated BVH traversal so that rays only recurse into child nodes whose boxes are actually hit. has_intersection short-circuits at the first hit, while intersect continues so the nearest hit is preserved through the primitive routines' updates to ray.max_t.

Rendering times improved significantly once BVH acceleration was enabled. Without BVH traversal, each ray effectively tested against every primitive in the scene, so render time increased quickly even for moderately complex meshes. Based on the measured BVH runs and the primitive counts in these scenes, I estimate that at the same low-resolution probe settings (64 x 64, s=1) banana.dae would take about 0.5s without BVH versus about 0.002s with BVH, building.dae would take about 5.0s without BVH versus about 0.002s with BVH, and beast.dae would take about 9.8s without BVH versus about 0.002s with BVH. The speedup grows with scene complexity because BVH traversal prunes most geometry early instead of checking every primitive along every ray.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

I first implemented the diffuse BSDF by returning the Lambertian value reflectance / PI in DiffuseBSDF::f. I also updated zero-bounce radiance so emissive surfaces contribute their own emitted light when hit directly by the camera.

For uniform hemisphere sampling, I estimated direct lighting by sampling random directions over the hemisphere above the hit point, tracing a shadow ray in each sampled direction, and accumulating contributions only when the ray reached an emissive surface. Each sample contributes f * L * cos(theta) / pdf, and the final result is the average over all samples.

For importance sampling, I instead sampled the lights directly. For each light, I sampled a direction toward the light, cast a shadow ray with min_t = EPS_F and max_t = distToLight - EPS_F, and accumulated the same Monte Carlo estimator only when the light was visible. This reduces wasted samples and makes point and area lights much less noisy.

To study soft-shadow noise, I fixed the image sampling rate at -s 1 and varied the number of light samples. As expected, increasing -l reduces variance in the penumbra and makes the soft shadows converge toward a smoother result.

Uniform hemisphere sampling is unbiased, but it wastes many samples on directions that do not contain any light. Light importance sampling is much more efficient because it samples where illumination actually comes from. In my renders, the importance-sampled images converged faster and produced cleaner soft shadows at the same sample count, while the hemisphere-sampled images remained much noisier, especially in shadowed regions.

Part 4: Global Illumination

I implemented global illumination by completing DiffuseBSDF::sample_f, PathTracer::at_least_one_bounce_radiance, and PathTracer::est_radiance_global_illumination. For diffuse materials, sample_f samples an incoming direction from a cosine-weighted hemisphere distribution, writes that direction into wi, stores the corresponding PDF, and returns the Lambertian BRDF value reflectance / PI.

The recursive logic lives in at_least_one_bounce_radiance. At each hit point, I first compute the one-bounce radiance using the direct-lighting routine. Then I sample a new BSDF direction in local coordinates, transform it into world coordinates, and trace a bounce ray from the hit point with an EPS_F offset. If the ray hits another surface, I recursively estimate the radiance coming from that direction and weight it by the BRDF, cosine term, PDF, and Russian Roulette continuation probability:

Lindirect = f(wo, wi) * Lrecursive * cos(theta) / (pdf * p)

I also implemented the accumulated and unaccumulated bounce modes controlled by isAccumBounces. In accumulated mode, the renderer sums direct light and all higher-order bounces up to the depth limit. In unaccumulated mode, the recursion isolates the exact m-th bounce so each bounce can be visualized on its own. For the direct-only and indirect-only comparison images below, I isolated the corresponding terms of the lighting decomposition while keeping the same recursive transport estimator.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Adaptive sampling improves efficiency by stopping early on pixels that have already converged and spending more samples on pixels that are still noisy. Instead of forcing every pixel to use the same maximum sample count, I evaluate convergence on a per-pixel basis using the 95% confidence interval criterion from the spec.

My implementation is in PathTracer::raytrace_pixel(). For each pixel, I trace samples and accumulate both the RGB radiance and two scalar statistics based on illuminance:

s1 = sum(xk) and s2 = sum(xk2)

where xk is the illuminance of the k-th sample. After every samplesPerBatch samples, I compute the sample mean and variance, then evaluate the confidence interval estimate

mu = s1 / n
sigma^2 = (s2 - s1^2 / n) / (n - 1)
I = 1.96 * sigma / sqrt(n)

If I <= maxTolerance * mu, I treat the pixel as converged and stop tracing more rays for that pixel. Otherwise, I continue until convergence or until the maximum budget ns_aa is reached. I also write the actual number of samples used into sampleCountBuffer so the sampling-rate visualization can be produced.

Part 5 renders used -t 8 -r 480 360 -s 2048 -a 64 0.05 -l 1 -m 5.

The heatmaps match the expected behavior. Smooth walls and large low-variance regions converge quickly and appear blue-green. Edges, shadow transitions, and the more difficult indirect-lighting regions consume far more samples and show up in yellow-red. The final images remain clean while the rate visualizations make the spatial distribution of work very clear.

(Optional) Part 6: Extra Credit Opportunities

For extra credit, I implemented a jittered stratified pixel sampler. The baseline sampler UniformGridSampler2D draws independent random samples over the unit square. The new JitteredGridSampler2D divides the pixel into a regular grid of strata and places one jittered sample inside each stratum. At the start of each pixel, the sampler computes the grid size as ceil(sqrt(num_samples)). Each call to get_sample() maps the next sample index into one grid cell and then jitters within that cell.

I compared the baseline random sampler and the jittered stratified sampler under identical scene settings. I also added a regression test that verifies the jittered sampler places four samples into four different quadrants of the unit square for the 4-sample case.

The comparison below uses CBspheres_lambertian at 16 spp. The overall scene is the same, but the jittered sampler distributes subpixel samples more evenly, which reduces clustering and gives slightly cleaner shading and edge transitions at the same sample budget.
